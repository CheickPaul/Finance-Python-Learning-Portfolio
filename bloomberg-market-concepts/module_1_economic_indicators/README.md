
# Module 1 — Economic Indicators

## A1. The Primacy of GDP / Economic Indicator Intro

GDP is often used as the economy’s “temperature gauge” and a natural starting point for macro analysis. It summarizes the size and performance of an economy and influences many investment decisions.

### 1) Why it matters for an investor

1) Earnings growth  
When economic growth strengthens, corporate revenues and profits tend to improve.

2) Inflation pressure  
Strong demand combined with limited capacity can push prices higher.

3) Interest rates  
Central banks typically react to the business cycle. Strong growth and rising inflation risk can lead to tighter policy (rates up). Weakening growth and cooling inflation can lead to easier policy (rates down).

4) Risk and sentiment  
Softer growth can increase recession concerns and raise risk premia in markets.

### 2) How investors read GDP growth

Investors do not focus only on the headline number. They care about:

1) Direction  
Is growth accelerating or slowing?

2) Pace  
Is growth healthy, or too fast (overheating risk)?

3) Quality (where growth comes from)  
Consumption and whether it looks sustainable.  
Investment (capex and real estate).  
Government spending.  
Net exports.

4) Real vs nominal GDP  
Nominal GDP includes inflation (prices and volumes).  
Real GDP removes inflation and is typically more useful to assess “true” activity.

### 3) How they forecast GDP (forecasting)

Markets try to anticipate GDP before the official release by watching a dashboard of indicators.

1) Leading indicators (signal what is ahead)  
Examples include new orders, confidence indicators, yield-curve or rate-curve signals, and building permits.

2) Coincident indicators (confirm current conditions)  
Examples include industrial production, employment, and income.

3) Lagging indicators (confirm after the move)  
Examples include some labor and cost measures that react late in the cycle.

The goal is to detect turning points, when growth shifts from acceleration to slowdown, or the reverse.

### 4) What makes a good economic indicator

1) Reliable  
Clear methodology and harder to manipulate.

2) Timely  
Released quickly.

3) Stable  
Limited revisions, or revisions that are predictable.

4) Representative  
Captures a meaningful part of the economy.

5) Useful for forecasting  
Helps anticipate the cycle and GDP momentum.

### 5) Example of top indicators followed in the US

1) Employment  
Payrolls and unemployment.

2) Inflation  
CPI and PCE.

3) Consumption  
Retail sales as a demand proxy.

4) Real activity  
Industrial production.

5) Housing  
Housing starts and building permits.

## A2. The Origin of GDP

### A2.1 Part 1

#### 1) GDP as a 20th-century invention

GDP became the standard measure of economic activity because it provides a single number that helps summarize:

1) The size of an economy  
2) Its growth over time  
3) A benchmark to compare countries, cycles, and policy choices

#### 2) Where did it come from?

It emerged from a practical need: measuring the economy consistently to guide public decisions.

Earlier data was fragmented. Over time, countries developed macro accounting frameworks that aggregate production and income over a period. This approach later became an international standard.

#### 3) World GDP (big picture)

Over the long run, global growth helps explain:

1) The long-run rise in corporate earnings  
2) The expansion of financial markets (equities, credit, and other assets)

### A2.2 The Origin of GDP — Part 2

We cannot directly observe the economy in real time. That is why investors rely on indicators such as GDP, inflation, and jobs as a dashboard to judge whether the economy is speeding up, slowing down, or approaching a turning point.

### A2.3 The Origin of GDP — Part 3

GDP and macro statistics expanded because policymakers learned that without solid data, managing the economy is like driving with poor visibility.

#### 1) The nation-state framework

Most macro data is organized by country:

1) Activity is measured at the national level  
2) Governments set fiscal frameworks  
3) Central banks set monetary policy  
4) Laws, taxes, and currencies are typically national

#### 2) The Roaring 1920s → policy mistakes → Great Depression

The 1920s saw strong growth and inflation, but also major misjudgments. Poor measurement can lead to reacting too late or reacting in the wrong way. This increased the demand for robust statistics to guide decisions.

#### 3) WWII → urgent demand for better data

War planning required reliable measures to answer concrete questions:

1) How much can the economy produce at maximum capacity?  
2) Which resources are missing?  
3) Where are the bottlenecks?
